The Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) is a parallel federal income tax system designed to ensure that high-income taxpayers pay a minimum level of tax regardless of the deductions and exclusions available under the regular tax system. Taxpayers must calculate their liability under both systems and pay whichever amount is higher. For equity compensation planning, the AMT is most relevant to holders of Incentive Stock Options.

Under the AMT system, certain items that are excluded from regular taxable income are added back as AMT preference items or adjustments. The spread on ISO exercise, defined as the fair market value of the shares at exercise minus the strike price, is the most significant AMT adjustment for equity compensation recipients. The full spread is added to Alternative Minimum Taxable Income (AMTI) in the year of exercise, even if the shares have not been sold and no cash has been received.

For tax year 2024, the AMT exemption is eighty-five thousand seven hundred dollars for single filers and one hundred thirty-three thousand three hundred dollars for married couples filing jointly. These exemptions phase out at higher income levels, beginning at six hundred nine thousand three hundred fifty dollars for single filers and one million two hundred eighteen thousand seven hundred dollars for married filers. The exemption is reduced by twenty-five cents for every dollar of AMTI above the phase-out threshold.

The AMT rate is twenty-six percent on the first two hundred thirty-two thousand six hundred dollars of AMTI above the exemption, and twenty-eight percent on AMTI above that threshold. Because the AMT rate is lower than the top regular income tax rate of thirty-seven percent, the AMT generally becomes relevant for taxpayers in the middle income ranges rather than the highest earners, who tend to owe more under the regular system regardless.

When a taxpayer pays AMT in a given year, they accumulate a Minimum Tax Credit, reported on Form 8801. This credit can be carried forward indefinitely and used to offset regular tax liability in future years when the taxpayer's regular tax exceeds their tentative minimum tax. In years when ISO shares are sold, the AMT adjustment from the prior exercise year reverses partially, which can reduce the AMT credit available.

To avoid large unexpected AMT bills, advisors recommend projecting the AMT impact before exercising ISOs, particularly when the spread is large. Strategies to manage AMT exposure include exercising ISOs early in the tax year to allow time to reassess, exercising in stages across multiple years, selling some ISO shares in the same year as exercise to generate cash for any resulting AMT liability, and targeting exercise in years when other income is lower. A sell-in-same-year strategy eliminates AMT on those shares because a same-year sale is treated as a disqualifying disposition, converting AMT exposure into ordinary income instead.
