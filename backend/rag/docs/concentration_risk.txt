Concentration risk in the context of equity compensation refers to the danger of holding too large a proportion of one's total net worth in a single company's stock. Financial planners widely use a threshold of fifteen percent as the maximum advisable allocation to any single position. Exceeding this threshold exposes the investor to company-specific risk that cannot be diversified away, meaning that adverse events affecting only that one company can cause disproportionate damage to overall wealth.

The risk is particularly acute for employees holding equity compensation in their employer's stock. This creates a dual concentration problem: the employee's employment income and their investment portfolio are both tied to the same company's fortunes. If the employer encounters financial difficulty, the employee may face simultaneous job loss and a decline in the value of their equity holdings, compounding the financial impact far beyond what would affect a diversified investor.

The fifteen percent threshold is measured as the current market value of the concentrated position divided by total investable net worth. For equity compensation, this calculation should include both vested shares already held and the intrinsic value of exercisable options, since those represent wealth that is exposed to the company's performance even before exercise. Some advisors use a more conservative ten percent threshold for employer stock specifically, given the dual concentration dynamic.

Several strategies are available for reducing single-stock concentration while managing tax costs. Systematic selling involves establishing a regular schedule of sales, spreading the tax liability over multiple years and avoiding the psychological difficulty of timing the market. A Rule 10b5-1 plan allows company insiders to establish a prearranged trading plan during an open trading window, enabling sales to occur automatically on future dates without triggering insider trading concerns. Exchange funds allow investors to contribute concentrated stock positions to a partnership in exchange for a diversified interest, deferring capital gains tax, though these vehicles have strict requirements and are typically available only to accredited investors.

Charitable strategies offer another avenue for managing concentration while achieving philanthropic goals. Donating appreciated shares directly to a donor-advised fund allows the donor to claim a charitable deduction at the current fair market value while avoiding capital gains tax on the appreciation. A charitable remainder trust can provide a stream of income while transferring the concentrated position to charity at death, also deferring capital gains.

For employees with unvested RSUs or unexercised options, the path to diversification involves both the timing of exercise decisions and post-exercise sales. Selling shares immediately upon RSU vesting eliminates future concentration growth from that tranche. For options, exercising and simultaneously selling shares converts the equity compensation value into diversified assets, though this foregoes any future upside and has immediate tax consequences. The optimal balance between holding for potential appreciation and selling to reduce concentration is unique to each client's risk tolerance, time horizon, and tax situation.
