Incentive Stock Options (ISOs) are a form of equity compensation available exclusively to employees of the granting company. They are governed by Section 422 of the Internal Revenue Code and carry significant tax advantages compared to non-qualified stock options, provided specific holding period requirements are met.

At grant and at exercise, ISOs generate no regular federal income tax liability. The employee pays the strike price to acquire shares, but the spread between the strike price and the fair market value at exercise is not subject to ordinary income tax. However, that same spread is classified as an AMT preference item and must be included in the calculation of Alternative Minimum Tax. This is the primary tax risk associated with ISO exercise.

To receive the most favorable tax treatment, an employee must satisfy two holding period conditions. First, the shares must be held for more than two years from the original grant date. Second, the shares must be held for more than one year from the exercise date. If both conditions are met, the eventual sale of the stock is treated as a long-term capital gain, taxed at rates of 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on the taxpayer's income. This outcome is called a qualifying disposition.

If either holding period is not met, the result is a disqualifying disposition. In that case, the lesser of the gain at exercise or the total gain at sale is treated as ordinary income in the year of sale. Any remaining gain above the spread is treated as a capital gain, which may be short-term or long-term depending on how long the shares were held after exercise. Disqualifying dispositions allow the employer to take a corresponding tax deduction equal to the ordinary income recognized by the employee.

There is an annual limit on the value of ISOs that can become exercisable in any single calendar year. The total fair market value of shares underlying ISOs that first become exercisable in a given year cannot exceed one hundred thousand dollars, measured at the grant date. Options exceeding this threshold are automatically treated as NSOs for that calendar year.

ISOs must be exercised within ten years of the grant date. Upon termination of employment, ISOs must generally be exercised within three months of the last day of employment to retain their ISO status. After that window, exercising the option results in NSO treatment, eliminating the potential for qualifying disposition treatment.

Because the AMT exposure from a large ISO exercise can be substantial, advisors typically recommend modeling the AMT impact before exercise, considering multi-year exercise strategies, and timing exercises to years with lower ordinary income where AMT exemptions provide greater relief. Spreading ISO exercises across multiple tax years is one of the most effective strategies for managing cumulative AMT liability.
