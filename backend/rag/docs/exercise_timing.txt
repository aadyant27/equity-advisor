The timing of stock option exercise is one of the most consequential decisions in equity compensation planning. It determines the tax treatment, the amount of AMT exposure, the holding period clock for capital gains, and the degree of concentration risk in the employee's portfolio. Several factors must be weighed simultaneously when evaluating when to exercise.

Expiry urgency is the most non-negotiable factor. Most stock options expire ten years from the grant date, and options that are in the money and approaching expiration must be exercised or they will be forfeited entirely. In the final twelve months before expiration, advisors should flag the grant as requiring immediate attention. Options expiring within ninety days represent a critical situation requiring same-month action. Upon termination of employment, ISOs must be exercised within three months to preserve their ISO status, and many NSO grants have even shorter post-termination exercise windows of thirty or sixty days specified in the grant agreement.

Blackout periods are mandatory trading restrictions that prevent employees with access to material non-public information from buying or selling company stock. These periods typically occur in the four to six weeks before the company's quarterly earnings release and end shortly after results are published. Employees must plan exercises to avoid blackout windows, which can create timing pressure when an expiration date falls within or immediately after a blackout period. Establishing a 10b5-1 plan during an open window allows future exercises and sales to occur automatically, bypassing blackout constraints.

Year-end tax planning creates natural pressure to exercise before December 31 or to defer until January. Exercising NSOs before year-end accelerates ordinary income into the current tax year, which is advantageous if the employee expects to be in a higher bracket next year. Deferring to January delays the tax liability by twelve months. For ISOs, exercising in early January of a given year gives the employee the maximum runway to assess cumulative AMT exposure before the tax filing deadline, whereas exercising in December leaves little time to respond to an unexpected AMT bill.

The relationship between the current fair market value and the strike price determines the intrinsic value of an option. Deep in-the-money options with high intrinsic value and short time to expiry carry the greatest urgency. Out-of-the-money options have no intrinsic value and should generally not be exercised, as paying the strike price for shares worth less in the market produces an immediate loss. At-the-money options present a judgment call based on the employee's outlook for the stock and their tax situation.

Spreading exercises across multiple tax years is the primary strategy for managing both AMT exposure on ISOs and bracket creep on NSOs. Rather than exercising a large grant in a single year, exercising a fraction each year keeps the annual income increment smaller, reduces AMT preference items below the exemption threshold, and smooths the tax impact over time. This approach requires careful planning but typically produces a lower total tax cost compared to a single large exercise event.
