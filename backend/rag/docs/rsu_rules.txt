Restricted Stock Units (RSUs) are a form of equity compensation in which the employer promises to deliver shares of company stock to the employee upon satisfaction of a vesting schedule. Unlike stock options, RSUs have no strike price and require no purchase by the employee. Every vested RSU converts to one share of stock, making them valuable as long as the stock has any positive price at all.

The tax event for RSUs occurs at vesting, not at grant. On each vesting date, the fair market value of the shares delivered is recognized as ordinary income. This income is subject to federal and state income tax at the employee's marginal rates and is also subject to FICA payroll taxes, including Social Security up to the annual wage base and Medicare tax without limit. For high earners, an additional 0.9% Medicare surtax applies on wages above two hundred thousand dollars for single filers or two hundred fifty thousand dollars for married filers.

Because RSUs produce ordinary income automatically at vesting, the employer is required to withhold taxes. The most common withholding method is sell-to-cover, in which the company sells a portion of the vesting shares on the employee's behalf to generate cash sufficient to cover the tax withholding obligation. Some companies offer net settlement, retaining shares equal in value to the tax withholding rather than selling them. Employees may also be permitted to pay the withholding liability in cash, allowing them to retain all vesting shares.

The 83(b) election, which allows employees to elect taxation at grant rather than at vesting for restricted stock, is not available for RSUs. The IRS has ruled that RSUs do not constitute property for purposes of Section 83 until the shares are actually delivered. Therefore, employees cannot accelerate the tax recognition date on RSUs through an 83(b) election.

After vesting and delivery, the employee's tax basis in the shares equals the fair market value on the vesting date, which is the amount taxed as ordinary income. Any subsequent gain or loss is treated as capital gain or loss when the shares are sold. Shares held for more than one year after the vesting date qualify for long-term capital gains rates. Shares sold within one year of vesting produce short-term capital gain taxed as ordinary income.

If an employee terminates employment before RSUs vest, the unvested units are generally forfeited with no compensation and no tax consequence. Some grants include acceleration provisions triggered by change of control events or involuntary termination, which can cause a large block of RSUs to vest simultaneously, creating a significant tax event in a single year.

RSU planning typically focuses on managing the timing and size of vesting events relative to other income, deciding whether to hold or sell shares immediately at vesting, and considering whether the stock is worth holding given concentration risk in the employer's stock.
