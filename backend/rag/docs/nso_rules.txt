Non-Qualified Stock Options (NSOs), also called non-statutory stock options, are the most common form of stock option and can be granted to employees, directors, consultants, and other service providers. Unlike ISOs, NSOs carry no special tax advantages under the Internal Revenue Code, but they also carry no AMT risk and no restrictions on grant size or recipient eligibility.

No tax event occurs at grant. The option recipient simply holds the right to purchase shares at the strike price during the option term, which is typically ten years. The tax consequence is deferred entirely to the point of exercise.

At exercise, the spread between the fair market value of the shares on the exercise date and the strike price is recognized as ordinary income. This amount is subject to federal and state income tax at the recipient's marginal rates, and for employees it is also subject to payroll taxes including Social Security and Medicare. The employer is required to withhold income tax and FICA taxes from employees at exercise, typically satisfied through a net exercise, a same-day sale, or a cash payment by the employee. The employer simultaneously recognizes a tax deduction equal to the ordinary income reported by the employee, which is one reason NSOs are often preferred by companies over ISOs.

After exercise, the employee's tax basis in the acquired shares equals the fair market value on the exercise date, which is the amount already taxed as ordinary income. Any subsequent appreciation or depreciation in the share price is treated as capital gain or loss when the shares are eventually sold. If the shares are held for more than one year after exercise, the gain is long-term and taxed at preferential capital gains rates of 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income. If sold within one year of exercise, the gain is short-term and taxed as ordinary income.

There is no holding period requirement to receive any particular tax treatment on NSOs beyond the standard one-year long-term capital gain threshold that applies to all capital assets. There is no two-year-from-grant requirement as with ISOs. An employee can exercise and immediately sell NSO shares without triggering any disqualifying disposition rules; the tax treatment is simply ordinary income on the spread at exercise and potentially no further gain if sold the same day.

NSOs that are out of the money have no intrinsic value but may still have time value. Options deep in the money with little time remaining before expiration should be evaluated for immediate exercise, particularly if the employee expects the stock to continue appreciating. The key planning consideration for NSOs is the timing of exercise relative to the employee's other income, since the spread adds directly to adjusted gross income and can push the employee into higher marginal tax brackets or phase out deductions and credits.
